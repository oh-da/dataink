# Audience and Context Evaluation

Success in data visualization does not start with the visualization itself; it starts with a deep understanding of the situational context. Before generating any infographic or chart, you must be able to clearly articulate exactly who you are communicating to and what you want them to know or do. 

## 1. WHO is your audience?
The more specific you can be about your audience, the better positioned you will be to successfully communicate. 
* **Avoid general audiences:** Do not target "anyone who might be interested" or "internal and external stakeholders." Trying to communicate to disparate needs at once means you cannot effectively communicate to any of them.
* **Identify the decision-maker:** Narrow your audience down to the specific person or group that can actually drive the outcome you seek.
* **Consider the relationship:** Determine if you have an established relationship with this audience. Do they already trust you as an expert, or do you need to establish credibility? This will dictate the flow of your story and how much supporting data is needed.

## 2. WHAT do you need them to know or do?
You should always want your audience to know or do something; if you cannot concisely articulate this, you should revisit whether you need to communicate in the first place. 
* **Recommend an action:** As the person analyzing the data, you are the subject matter expert and are in a unique position to interpret it. Confidently recommend an action rather than leaving the audience to figure out what to do.
* **Prompting words:** Use strong action verbs to define your goal (e.g., *accept, agree, change, defend, implement, invest, persuade, support*). 

## 3. The 3-Minute Story
Once you know the *Who* and the *What*, you must boil down the "so-what" of your communication. The 3-minute story is a planning exercise where you challenge yourself to articulate the core narrative in exactly three minutes.
* This forces you to deeply understand your material and strip away non-essential details.
* It removes your reliance on the visual tools, ensuring you understand the underlying story first.

## 4. The Big Idea
To achieve ultimate clarity, boil the 3-minute story down even further into the "Big Idea". A successful Big Idea has three mandatory components:
1. It must articulate your unique point of view.
2. It must convey what is at stake.
3. It must be a complete, single sentence.

*Example of a Big Idea:* "The pilot summer learning program was successful at improving students' perceptions of science and, because of this success, we recommend continuing to offer it going forward; please approve our budget for this program".

## Questions to Ask for Context
If you are designing an infographic based on someone else's request, ask these questions to tease out the necessary context before you begin storyboarding:
* Who is the decision maker and what do we know about them?
* What biases does our audience have that might make them resistant to our message?
* What data is available to strengthen our case, and is the audience already familiar with it?
* What would a successful outcome look like?
