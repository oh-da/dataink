# Brand Fonts and Typography Strategy

When applying corporate brand guidelines to data visualizations, the primary goal of your typography is legibility. If the text is hard to read, your audience will perceive the visualization as too difficult to understand and will be less likely to spend time engaging with it.

## 2. Prioritize Legibility and Consistency
*   **Keep it clean:** Always use a consistent, easy-to-read font. Avoid "fussy" or overly stylized fonts, as they add unnecessary cognitive burden and make the information feel complicated.
*   **Do not mix fonts for emphasis:** Avoid using different fonts as a highlighting technique, as it is difficult to attain a noticeable difference without disrupting the overall aesthetics.

## 3. Highlighting and Text Styling
*   **Use Bold for Emphasis:** Bolding is generally preferred over italics and underlining because it adds minimal noise to the design while clearly highlighting chosen elements like titles, labels, captions, and short word sequences.
*   **Use Italics Sparingly:** Italics add minimal noise, but they do not stand out as much as bold text and are generally less legible.
*   **Avoid Underlining:** Underlining adds visual noise and compromises legibility, so it should be used sparingly, if at all.
*   **Strategic Uppercase:** Uppercase text in short word sequences is easily scanned, which can work well when applied to graph titles, axis labels, and keywords.

## 4. Size and Visual Hierarchy
*   **Use Size for Importance:** Size is an effective preattentive attribute used to attract attention and quickly signal relative importance.
*   **Standardize Base Text Size:** Generally, all text in the visual should be the same size except where intentional decisions are made to establish a visual hierarchy.
*   **Make Titles Larger:** Graph titles should be larger than the rest of the text to ensure they are read first.
*   **De-emphasize Footnotes:** Push necessary but non-message-impacting details (like data sources) to the background via smaller font sizes and a low-priority placement at the bottom of the visual.
