## 2. The "Audience-Look-Here" Cue
Companies often have extensive color palettes, but you should not use them all at once in a single graph.
*   **Use Color Sparingly:** Identify just one or two brand-appropriate colors to serve as your "audience-look-here" cues. 
*   **Highlight Strategically:** Use these bold brand colors strictly to highlight the most important parts of the visual. Keep in mind that the more things you make different, the lesser the degree to which any of them stand out. Too much variety prevents anything from standing out and creates an ineffective "rainbow" effect.

## 3. Mute the Baseline
*   Keep the remainder of the chart's color palette relatively muted.
*   Standardize on soft, natural colors like shades of grey for baseline data, axes, and non-essential information. This ensures the highlighted brand colors easily "pop" and the graph doesn't become visually overwhelming.

## 4. Ensure Sufficient Contrast
Sometimes a brand's primary color does not work well for highlighting data.
*   For example, if the primary brand color is a light shade of green, it may not be attention-grabbing enough against a white background, causing the visual to look washed out.
*   If this happens, deviate slightly by using a bold black or a complementary dark color to draw attention, provided it does not clash with the brand's logo or overall template.

## 5. Account for Dark Brand Templates
If the brand style guide mandates a dark or black background, the rules of visual contrast reverse:
*   Black becomes the baseline, grey stands out less, and white or bright colors (like yellow) become highly attention-grabbing.
*   Avoid using dark colors for data on a dark background; ensure light elements are used to create strong contrast, while being careful not to make the chart overly heavy or hard to read.
