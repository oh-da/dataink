 Visual Perception & Design Principles

When communicating with data, every single element you add to a page takes up cognitive load on the part of your audience. To make visuals as easy to interpret as possible, you must purposefully design the components to eliminate clutter and establish a clear visual hierarchy.

## 1. Maximize the Data-Ink Ratio
According to Edward Tufte, you should maximize the "data-ink ratio"—the larger the share of a graphic's ink devoted to data, the better.
*   **Remove non-data ink:** Identify unnecessary elements that take up space without adding informative value and remove them. This includes heavy chart borders, background shading, and gridlines.
*   **De-emphasize the baseline:** Push necessary but non-message-impacting items (like axes) to the background using light grey so they do not compete with the data.

## 2. Apply Gestalt Principles of Visual Perception
Leverage how people naturally interact with visual stimuli to organize data and reduce cognitive load.
*   **Proximity:** People perceive objects that are physically close together as belonging to a group. Place data labels directly next to the data they describe to eliminate the need for your audience to do the work of looking back and forth at a separate legend.
*   **Similarity:** Objects of similar color or shape are perceived as related. Make text labels the exact same color as the line or bar they describe to visually tie them together.
*   **Closure:** People naturally prefer simplicity and will mentally fill in visual gaps. Because of this, chart borders and background shading are unnecessary clutter; removing them allows your data to stand out more.
*   **Enclosure:** Light background shading is often enough to visually group enclosed elements, such as separating historical actuals from future forecasts.

## 3. Establish a Visual Hierarchy
Create implicit instructions for your audience, indicating the exact order in which they should process the information.
*   **Preattentive Attributes:** Utilize attributes like size, color, and position to draw attention. Our brains quickly pick up these visual differences without conscious thought.
*   **The 10% Rule:** Highlight a maximum of 10% of the visual design. Highlighting effects are diluted as the percentage of highlighted elements increases; the more things you make different, the lesser the degree to which any of them stand out.
*   **Size:** Relative size denotes relative importance. If you have one truly important takeaway, signal this by making it large.
*   **Position on Page:** Audiences naturally start at the top left of a page and scan in zigzagging "Z" shapes. Put your most important message or main takeaway in this precious real estate so the audience doesn't have to wade through other information to find it.

## 4. Alignment and White Space
Thoughtful design fades into the background so the audience doesn't even notice it; poor design feels sloppy and unorganized.
*   **Alignment:** Avoid center-aligned text and diagonal elements (like diagonal x-axis labels, which are significantly harder to read). Instead, upper-left-most justify your text (titles, axis labels, legends) to create clean vertical and horizontal lines. This ensures the audience hits the details that tell them how to read the graph before they get to the data itself.
*   **White Space:** Do not fear white space, and never add data just for the sake of filling empty space. Preserve margins and use white space strategically to draw attention to the elements on the page that are *not* white space.
