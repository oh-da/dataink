# Logic Validation Tactics

To ensure that the story you are telling comes across clearly in your presentation or report, use the following validation tactics. These checks act as a final review of your narrative structure before you deliver it to your audience.

## 1. Horizontal Logic
The idea behind horizontal logic is that you should be able to read *only* the slide titles (or section headers) from beginning to end, and those snippets alone will tell the complete, overarching story. 
*   **Action Titles:** This tactic only works if you use clear action titles rather than static, descriptive titles. 
*   **Executive Summary Alignment:** A great way to set this up is to include an executive summary slide at the beginning. Each bullet point on that summary should correspond directly to a subsequent slide title in the exact same order.

## 2. Vertical Logic
Vertical logic ensures that all information on a single slide or page is self-reinforcing. 
*   The content must directly reinforce the title, and the title must accurately reflect the content.
*   The words must reinforce the visual (chart, graph, or table), and the visual must reinforce the words.
*   **Eliminate the Extraneous:** There should be no unrelated information on the page. If a data point, bullet, or fact does not support the main point of that specific slide, either eliminate it entirely or push it to an appendix.

## 3. Reverse Storyboarding
While storyboarding happens at the very beginning of the planning process, reverse storyboarding is used at the end to test your final flow.
*   Take your completed presentation or communication, flip through it page by page, and write down the single main point of each page.
*   The resulting list should perfectly match the original storyboard or outline you intended to convey.
*   If the list does not match, this exercise highlights exactly where structurally you need to add, remove, or reorder pieces to fix the narrative flow.

## 4. Seek a Fresh Perspective
When you are the one analyzing the data and building the presentation, you become highly familiar with the content—sometimes too familiar to realize when something isn't obvious.
*   Show your completed communication to someone who is not as familiar with the topic.
*   Ask them to explain the main takeaways back to you without your voiceover. 
*   If they stumble, have questions, or draw the wrong conclusion, you have identified areas where the narrative logic or visual clarity needs to be improved.
