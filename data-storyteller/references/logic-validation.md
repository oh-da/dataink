# Logic Validation Tactics

To ensure that your story comes across clearly, use the following validation tactics as a final review of your narrative structure before delivery.

> *The Horizontal Logic, Vertical Logic, and Reverse Storyboarding techniques described below were developed by Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic and are presented in* Storytelling with Data *(Wiley, 2015), Chapter 8.*

## 1. Horizontal Logic
Extract every slide title (or section header) from your deck and read them in sequence as a standalone paragraph. If that paragraph tells a coherent, complete story on its own, the structure is sound. If there are gaps or non-sequiturs, the narrative needs restructuring.
*   **Action Titles:** This only works when titles state a conclusion or insight (e.g., "Sales dropped 12% due to stockouts") rather than a generic topic label (e.g., "Q3 Sales").
*   **Executive Summary Alignment:** An executive summary slide at the start should mirror the title sequence — each bullet mapping to a subsequent slide in the same order.

## 2. Vertical Logic
Within any single slide or page, every element should support the same point. The title states the insight; the chart proves it; the annotation explains it. Nothing should be present that does not directly reinforce that one message.
*   If a data point, bullet, or fact does not support the slide's main point, move it to an appendix or remove it entirely.

## 3. Reverse Storyboarding
After completing your deck, go through it page by page and jot down the single takeaway from each page. Compare that list against your original outline.
*   Where the list matches, the narrative is working.
*   Where it diverges, you have identified the pages that need to be added, removed, or reordered.

## 4. Seek a Fresh Perspective
When you have been deep in the analysis, you can become too close to the material to spot unclear logic.
*   Show the finished communication to someone unfamiliar with the topic and ask them to summarize the main takeaways without your voiceover.
*   Anywhere they stumble, misinterpret, or draw a blank is a signal that the narrative logic or visual clarity needs tightening.
