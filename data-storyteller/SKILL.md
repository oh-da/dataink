# Data Storytelling Expert

-# Performance Notes
- Do not start by choosing charts; always start by defining the story's structure.
- The story is not about the data; the story is about the audience. 

## CRITICAL CONSTRAINTS
- The audience **MUST** be treated as the main character (the protagonist) of the story. 
- Every story **MUST** have a clear call to action; do not just present data and leave the audience to figure out what to do.
- Every slide or section **MUST** use an action title, not a descriptive title.

## Instructions

### Step 1: Define the Audience and the Tension
Identify the protagonist (the audience) and the specific problem they face. 
- Frame the story around the conflict between "what is" and "what could be" to immediately give the audience a stake in the solution.

### Step 2: Construct the Three-Act Story Arc
Organize the communication into a clear beginning, middle, and end.
- **The Beginning:** Set the stage, introduce the plot and the imbalance (the problem), and answer "Why should I pay attention?".
- **The Middle:** Use data as supporting evidence to further develop the problem, explore options, and convince the audience to accept the proposed solution. 
- **The End:** Wrap up with a clear, specific call to action.
*Consult `references/narrative-arc.md` for specific questions to answer in each act.*

### Step 3: Determine the Narrative Flow and Repetition
Decide on the path you will take the audience along.
- Choose between a **Chronological Flow** (to build trust and show the analytical process) or **Leading with the Ending** (for a busy audience that just wants the "so what").
- Apply the **"Bing, Bang, Bongo"** principle of repetition: tell them what you are going to tell them, tell them, and then summarize what you told them.
*Consult `references/flow-and-repetition.md` for execution details.*

### Step 4: Validate the Story using Logic Checks
Test the structure to ensure the message is undeniably clear.
- **Horizontal Logic:** Ensure that reading just the action titles of each slide/section tells the complete overarching story.
- **Vertical Logic:** Ensure all content on a single page directly reinforces the title, and vice versa.
- **Reverse Storyboarding:** Review the final outline by writing down the main point of each page to see if it perfectly matches the intended flow.
*Consult `references/logic-validation.md` for troubleshooting logic errors.*

## Examples

**Example 1: Structuring a Presentation**
* **User says:** "I have a lot of data showing our new product is doing well, but marketing spend is too high. Help me turn this into a slide deck for the executives."
* **Actions:** 
    1. Apply CRITICAL constraint: Ensure the executives are the hero and there is a specific call to action (e.g., "Reallocate $50k from marketing to product development").
    2. Draft the three acts: Beginning (Product success), Middle (Tension: High marketing costs are eating margins, shown via data), End (Call to action).
    3. Apply Horizontal Logic: Generate a list of 5-7 slide action titles that read as a cohesive paragraph.

# The Three-Act Narrative Arc

A successful data communication functions like a play or movie, requiring a setup, conflict, and resolution. 

**Act I: The Beginning (Setup)**
Get everyone on common ground. Address the following:
*   **The setting:** When and where does the story take place?.
*   **The main character:** Frame this in terms of your audience.
*   **The imbalance:** What has changed? Why is it necessary to communicate?.

**Act II: The Middle (Conflict)**
This makes up the bulk of your story. Retain attention by addressing how the audience can solve the problem.
*   Incorporate data that visually demonstrates the problem.
*   Articulate what will happen if no action is taken.
*   Illustrate the benefits of your recommended solution.

**Act III: The End (Resolution)**
The story must have a definitive end.
*   Tie it back to the beginning to recap the problem.
*   End with a very specific call to action—what do you want them to do with this new knowledge?.

# Narrative Flow and Repetition

**Narrative Flow**
The narrative flow is the path along which you take your audience. 
*   **Chronological:** Follow the analytical process (identify problem -> gather data -> analyze -> recommend). Best used when you need to establish credibility or if the audience cares deeply about the process.
*   **Lead with the Ending:** Start with the call to action, then back up into the supporting data. Best for busy audiences or when trust is already established. It immediately gives the audience a lens through which to view the rest of the presentation.

**The Power of Repetition**
Hearing, reading, and saying things repeatedly transfers them from short-term to long-term memory.
*   **Repeatable Sound Bites:** Create succinct, clear phrases that the audience can easily recall and transfer.
*   **Bing, Bang, Bongo:** 
    *   *Bing:* Use an executive summary to tell them what you're going to tell them.
    *   *Bang:* Provide the actual detailed data content.
    *   *Bongo:* Conclude with a summary slide that reviews the main points and repeats the call to action.

# Logic Validation Tactics

Use these three tactics to guarantee the clarity of the presentation deck or report:

**1. Horizontal Logic**
You should be able to read *only* the slide titles (or section headers) from beginning to end, and those snippets alone should tell the overarching story. This requires the strict use of action titles rather than descriptive titles (e.g., use "Sales declined in Q3 due to stockouts" instead of "Q3 Sales").

**2. Vertical Logic**
All information on a given slide must be self-reinforcing. The charts must reinforce the words, and the words must reinforce the chart. Any extraneous data or unrelated facts must be eliminated or pushed to the appendix.

**3. Reverse Storyboarding**
To test your final flow, take the completed communication, flip through it, and write down the single main point of each page. The resulting list should perfectly match your original intended storyboard. If it doesn't, you must restructure the narrative.
